\section*{Exercise 1.1 - Simulation of Dose Reduction}

\subsection*{Objective and setup}

The aim is to characterise how image quality degrades when both the photon
dose and the number of projection angles are reduced.
A chest CT image ($512{\times}512$) was used as the phantom, masked to its
inscribed reconstruction circle.
Three dose levels, $I_0 \in \{10^5, 10^3, 10^2\}$~photons, and three view
counts, $n \in \{360, 90, 20\}$, were combined to produce nine experimental
conditions.
Parallel-beam geometry with angles sampled uniformly over $[0^\circ, 180^\circ)$
was used throughout.

\subsection*{Noise model}

Clean sinograms were generated via the Radon transform.
Each was then corrupted in the transmission domain:
\begin{align}
  p_{\mathrm{norm}} &= p\,/\,p_{\max}, \label{eq:pnorm}\\
  I &= I_0\,\exp(-p_{\mathrm{norm}}), \label{eq:beer}\\
  I_g &= I + \mathcal{N}(0,\,\sigma^2), \quad \sigma = 0.05, \label{eq:gauss}\\
  I_{\mathrm{noisy}} &\sim \mathrm{Poisson}\!\bigl(\max(I_g,\,\varepsilon)\bigr), \label{eq:poisson}\\
  p_{\mathrm{noisy}} &= -\log\!\left(\tfrac{I_{\mathrm{noisy}}}{I_0}\right)\cdot p_{\max}. \label{eq:back}
\end{align}

Step~\eqref{eq:pnorm} normalises the sinogram before Beer-Lambert to keep
transmission counts in a numerically stable range (as suggested in the practical).
Step~\eqref{eq:gauss} applies Gaussian \emph{detector} noise first;
Step~\eqref{eq:poisson} then applies Poisson \emph{photon-counting} noise
to the perturbed counts --- this ordering was specified by the course instructor.
At $\sigma{=}0.05$ the Gaussian term is negligible relative to Poisson
fluctuations at all tested dose levels, so the dominant noise source is
Poisson throughout.

To first visualise the isolated effect of the detector-noise term, I also
generated an auxiliary set of sinograms using only the Gaussian step in
\eqref{eq:gauss}, omitting the Poisson sampling step. This diagnostic plot is
shown in Figure~\ref{fig:ex11_sino_gauss}; it is included only for
interpretation and is \emph{not} used in the reconstruction experiments.
The combined Gaussian+Poisson sinograms used in the actual experiments are then
shown in Figure~\ref{fig:ex11_sino}.

\subsection*{Reconstruction methods}

\paragraph{FBP.}
Ramp-filtered backprojection (\texttt{skimage.iradon},
\texttt{filter\_name="ramp"}) was applied directly to each noisy sinogram.
FBP is a non-iterative method that applies the Ram-Lak ramp filter in the
1D Fourier domain of each projection before backprojecting, implementing
the exact inversion formula from the Fourier Slice Theorem.

\paragraph{Gradient Descent}
The iterative update follows:
\[
  x^{k+1} = x^k - \gamma\,A^T\!(Ax^k - b), \qquad \gamma = 0.001,
\]
where $A = \mathrm{radon}(\cdot)$ and $A^T = \mathrm{iradon}(\cdot,\;
\texttt{filter\_name=None})$ is the unfiltered backprojection.
Initialisation is $x^0 = \mathbf{0}$; 200 iterations are run with no
regularisation or non-negativity projection.

\subsection*{Key findings}
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0pt}

  \item \textbf{GD outperforms FBP across all settings.}
        FBP performs best under ideal conditions
        ($I_0{=}10^5$, 360 views: 36.9~dB, SSIM 0.891),
        but degrades sharply with noise and undersampling
        (PSNR drop of $\sim$30~dB from $I_0{=}10^5$ to $10^2$).
        GD degrades more gradually (37.1$\rightarrow$17.3~dB),
        indicating greater robustness.

  \item \textbf{Strong compounding effect of low dose and sparse views.}
        In the worst case ($I_0{=}10^2$, 20 views),
        FBP collapses to $-5.67$~dB (no useful reconstruction),
        while GD still achieves 15.1~dB, showing it can partially
        compensate for both noise and undersampling.

  \item \textbf{Practical implication.}
        FBP requires high-dose, dense-view acquisitions for reliable quality.
        GD remains stable under reduced dose and sparse views,
        but at the cost of higher computational effort.

\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Methods to improve image quality}
\begin{enumerate}\setlength\itemsep{0pt}
  \item \textbf{Regularised iterative reconstruction.} Minimising
        $\|Ax-b\|^2 + \lambda R(x)$ with a Total Variation penalty
        ($R=\|\nabla x\|_1$) would suppress streak artefacts, preserve
        sharp edges, and eliminate the semi-convergence problem observed in unregularised GD. As literature mentions that in 
        low-dose CT reconstruction regularisation is a
        central strategy for balancing noise suppression and edge
        preservation \cite{zhang2018regularization}.

  \item \textbf{Sinogram pre-filtering.} Denoising the sinogram prior to
        FBP (e.g.\ with a low-pass filter or a learned denoiser) would
        reduce noise amplification by the ramp filter, at the cost of
        some spatial resolution. Sinogram-domain preprocessing has also
        been studied in CT as a way to improve reconstruction robustness
        before inversion \cite{meng2010sinogram}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Differences in the limited-angle case}

Compared to Exercise~1.1, limited-angle reconstruction
introduces a qualitatively different degradation.

In 1.1, reducing the number of projections mainly increases noise and streak
artefacts, but the overall anatomy remains recognisable even at low view counts.


In contrast, restricting the angular range produces strongly directional
artefacts. At 120° clear distortions appear, and at 40° the image becomes
severely elongated with loss of structural detail. 

This occurs because limited-angle data is incomplete: projections do not cover
the full angular range, leading to a missing wedge in Fourier space. 


\subsection*{Why limited-angle tomography fails}

I believe the key idea is the Fourier Slice Theorem. Each projection at angle $\theta$ samples one radial line in the 2D Fourier
domain.
If projections are available only over a limited arc $[0^\circ,\alpha]$,
then part of Fourier space remains unsampled, creating a \textit{missing wedge}.

I believe this is why limited-angle CT fails to reconstruct some edges well.
Edges correspond to high-frequency components perpendicular to the edge orientation
This likely explains the directional blur and elongation artefacts seen in
limited-angle reconstructions, which become especially severe at very narrow
ranges such as $40^\circ$. A more formal characterization of these
limited-angle artefacts, including which singularities remain visible and
which are lost, is given by Frikel and Quinto \cite{frikel2013limited}.

\subsection*{Part (a): FBP filters in the literature}

Besides the standard ramp filter, common FBP filters include
\textbf{Shepp--Logan}, \textbf{Cosine}, \textbf{Hann}, and \textbf{Hamming}.
All are windowed versions of the ramp filter. Comparative studies of
shaping filters in CT reconstruction discuss the same family of filters
and emphasize their noise--resolution trade-off \cite{prabhu2021filters}.

\subsection*{Part (c): Subset gradient descent vs full-batch gradient descent}

\paragraph{Methods.}
Full-batch GD uses all $N{=}360$ projections per iteration:
\[
  x^{k+1} = x^k - \gamma\,A^\top(Ax^k - b), \quad \gamma = 0.001.
\]
Subset GD splits them into $S{=}10$ interleaved subsets and applies one update per subset:
\[
  x^{k+1/S} = x^k - \gamma_s\, A_s^\top(A_s x^k - b_s).
\]
Both process all projections per epoch, but subset GD performs $S$ updates instead of one.

\paragraph{Step sizes.}
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0pt}
  \item $\gamma_s = \gamma/S$: matches total update per epoch.
  \item $\gamma_s = \gamma$: gives an effective step $S\gamma$ ($10\times$ larger).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Convergence comparison.}
Figure~\ref{fig:ex13c_traj} shows the 100-epoch PSNR trajectories.

\textbf{Scaled variant ($\gamma/S$).}  
Subset GD matches full-batch GD almost exactly, with identical peak
($\approx$26.4~dB at epoch~33--34) and final performance (22.08~dB),
as confirmed by Tables~\ref{tab:ex13c_main} and \ref{tab:ex13c_early}.
Under this scaling, both methods are effectively equivalent.

\textbf{Unscaled variant ($\gamma_s = \gamma$).}  
Using the same per-step size yields an effective step $S\gamma$,
giving much faster convergence: peak quality is reached in \textbf{3 epochs}
vs \textbf{33 epochs} for full-batch (Table~\ref{tab:ex13c_early}).
This acceleration comes at the cost of \emph{instability}, with PSNR
dropping to 7.91~dB by epoch~100.

Overall, subset GD does not improve the final solution, but shifts the
\emph{speed--stability trade-off}, requiring early stopping or step-size decay.

\subsection*{Part (d): Why PET often uses MLEM, while CT may use SIRT-like methods}

The preferred reconstruction method depends on the data model.
In PET, the measurements are photon counts and are naturally described by a
Poisson model, so methods such as \textbf{MLEM} and \textbf{OSEM} are often a
good fit; the classical maximum-likelihood formulation for emission
tomography is due to Shepp and Vardi \cite{shepp1982mlem}.

In CT, the measurements are transmission data that are usually log-transformed
into attenuation values.
After this, the problem is often treated with Gaussian or least-squares
approximations, so iterative methods such as \textbf{SIRT}-like updates can be
more natural than a Poisson-likelihood method applied directly to the
transformed data. A classic algebraic example is the SART method of Andersen
and Kak \cite{andersen1984sart}.

\subsection*{Coil combination using root-sum-of-squares}

I combined the six reconstructed coil images in image space using the
root-sum-of-squares (RSS) method:
\begin{equation}
  I_{\mathrm{RSS}}(x,y)=\sqrt{\sum_{c=1}^{N_c}\lvert I_c(x,y)\rvert^2},
  \qquad N_c=6.
  \label{eq:rss}
\end{equation}

In practice, I took the magnitude image from each coil, squared it, summed the
six squared magnitudes pixel-wise, and then took the square root to obtain a
single combined image.
This produced the RSS image shown in Figure~\ref{fig:ex21_rss}.

every individual coil are now clearly visible, demonstrating the SNR and
coverage benefit of multi-coil combination.}
\label{fig:ex21_rss}

\subsection*{Observations}

The log-scaled k-space plots show a brighter central region in all six coils,
indicating that most of the signal energy lies in the low spatial frequencies.
Towards the periphery, the magnitude drops quickly and a visible noise floor
appears.

After reconstruction, the magnitude image shows the knee anatomy,
whereas the phase image mainly shows wrapped phase variation and is less useful
for anatomical interpretation here.

The six coil magnitude images all show the same anatomy, but with different
brightness distributions and noise levels, so no single coil gives uniform
coverage of the full field of view.

After RSS combination, the final image becomes more balanced and the anatomy is
visible across the full knee.
Although some intensity variation remains, the RSS image provides the clearest
overall representation of the dataset.

Median and bilateral filtering act more locally, with bilateral preserving
edges better.

Unlike the image-space methods, the Butterworth filter is applied before
magnitude extraction, so both magnitude and phase are affected.

\subsubsection*{Further improvements}

More advanced methods could further improve image quality. Non-local means
could exploit self-similarity for stronger denoising, while total variation
could better preserve edges. Improvements may also come from more advanced
coil combination (e.g.\ sensitivity-weighted methods) or integrating
denoising directly into the reconstruction process.

For the data-driven example, I selected Alom et al., which is clearly a
deep learning segmentation paper: it proposes RU-Net and R2U-Net,
architectures that extend U-Net with recurrent convolutional layers and
residual connections, and learns the segmentation mapping from labelled
image--mask pairs.
For the processing-based example, I selected Kim et al., which is a
classical segmentation study: it relies on atlas construction, affine and
nonlinear spatial normalisation, similarity-based atlas selection, and
ROI transfer, without any machine learning or deep learning.

\subsection*{Part (b): Paper summaries and critical comparison}

\paragraph{Alom et al.\ (2019) -  Recurrent residual U-Net (R2U-Net).}
Alom et al.\ propose two deep learning architectures, RU-Net and
R2U-Net, for medical image segmentation.
The models are evaluated on retinal blood vessel segmentation, skin
lesion segmentation, and lung lesion segmentation.
The encoder--decoder U-Net structure is retained, but standard
convolutional blocks are replaced by recurrent convolutional layers; in
R2U-Net these are additionally wrapped in residual connections to
improve feature propagation and training stability.
The paper uses a patch-based strategy for retinal vessel segmentation
and an end-to-end image-based strategy for the skin and lung lesion
tasks.
Performance is assessed using accuracy, sensitivity, specificity, Dice,
Jaccard, and AUC.
The authors report that RU-Net and especially R2U-Net generally
outperform equivalent SegNet, U-Net, and ResU-Net baselines, often
with the same or fewer parameters.
Overall, this is best viewed as a method-development paper showing
that recurrent residual feature accumulation can improve semantic
segmentation across several medical imaging tasks.

\paragraph{Kim et al.\ (2019) -  Multi-atlas cardiac PET segmentation.}
Kim et al.\ propose a multi-atlas segmentation pipeline for automatic
segmentation of the left ventricular myocardium in dynamic cardiac PET.
The study uses PET/CT scans from twelve rats after left coronary artery
ligation and focuses on ROI definition for subsequent perfusion and
kinetic analysis.
Each atlas is created by manually segmenting the left ventricle on
reoriented short-axis PET images and dividing the myocardium into
angular segments.
For a new source image, the method performs affine and nonlinear
spatial normalisation to each candidate atlas, then selects the
best-matching atlas using voxel-wise Pearson correlation and RMSE,
before transferring the predefined ROIs.
Performance is evaluated using leave-one-out cross-validation and
compared with both manual segmentation and a conventional single-atlas
approach.
The paper reports that multi-atlas segmentation agrees more closely
with manual segmentation than the single-atlas method, achieving
$r^2 = 0.92$ versus $r^2 = 0.88$ for perfusion estimates, while also
reducing underestimation in high-perfusion regions.
Methodologically, this is a classical atlas-based and registration-based
segmentation pipeline rather than a learned predictive model.

\paragraph{Comparison.}

The two papers represent two distinct segmentation paradigms.
Alom et al.\ propose a \textit{data-driven semantic segmentation} method:
RU-Net and R2U-Net learn a pixel-wise mapping from annotated image--mask
pairs using an encoder--decoder architecture with skip connections,
recurrent convolutional layers, and residual links.
By contrast, Kim et al.\ use a \textit{processing-based atlas segmentation}
pipeline: a new PET image is spatially normalised to several manually
labelled atlases, the best-matching atlas is selected using similarity
measures, and its ROIs are transferred to the source image.
In course terms, the first is supervised feature learning, whereas the
second is classical registration-based segmentation with explicit
anatomical priors.

The methods differ in both the information they use and their dependence
on annotation.
R2U-Net learns appearance features such as edges, texture, and context
directly from labelled data, which makes it potentially well suited to
heterogeneous targets with complex boundaries.
However, its performance depends on the quality and representativeness of
the training set, and the paper does not test robustness to domain shift
or unseen acquisition settings.
The multi-atlas method also depends on manual annotation, but only to
construct a small atlas library rather than a large supervised training
set.
This is likely to be advantageous in small, specialised studies such as
the rodent cardiac PET setting of Kim et al., where expert atlas creation
is feasible but large labelled datasets are not.

Their likely failure modes are correspondingly different.
R2U-Net may generalise well when relevant variability is represented in
training, and its learned features make it more flexible than template
transfer for fine vessels or lesion boundaries.
Its weakness is likely to be reduced performance on images that differ
substantially from the training distribution.
The atlas method is less affected by training-set bias, but is strongly
dependent on registration quality and atlas representativeness.

A further contrast is interpretability and computational profile.
The multi-atlas pipeline is relatively transparent, because atlas
creation, registration, similarity scoring, and ROI transfer can all be
inspected directly.
R2U-Net is architecturally understandable, but its internal learned
features are less interpretable.
Computationally, the trade-off is reversed: multi-atlas segmentation
avoids training but requires repeated affine and nonlinear registration
at inference time, whereas R2U-Net requires expensive training but then
segments in a single forward pass.
Overall, Kim et al.\ appears better suited to controlled quantitative
imaging settings with limited variability and a need for interpretability,
whereas Alom et al.\ appears better suited to broader semantic
segmentation problems, provided sufficient labelled training data are
available.
I believe neither of them is universally superior; the better choice depends on segmentation problem, annotation
availability, anatomical variability, and whether interpretability or
predictive flexibility is the main priority.
\subsection*{Part (c): Proposed improvement to Alom et al.\ (2019)}

A few limitations in Alom et al.\ seem worth addressing, and a
combined approach might resolve several of them at once.
Perhaps the most straightforward improvement would be to replace the
plain skip-connection concatenation with attention-gated skip
connections \cite{oktay2018attention}.
As the paper notes, R2U-Net struggles to cleanly separate foreground
from background in tasks like retinal vessel segmentation, where
the target structures are thin and highly imbalanced against the
background.
Since all encoder activations are forwarded equally, irrelevant
background features are likely being propagated into the decoder
alongside the useful ones.
Attention gates could help here by learning soft weights over the
encoder feature maps, guided by the decoder context, so that
spatially irrelevant activations are suppressed before concatenation.
This kind of modification would also keep the recurrent residual
structure intact and should not substantially increase the parameter
count --- which seems important given the paper's emphasis on
parameter efficiency.

A second issue that seems addressable is the blurred segmentation
boundaries visible in the qualitative results (Fig.~12 of the paper),
where model outputs consistently appear less sharp than the ground
truth.
This might be partly a loss function problem: Dice and cross-entropy
optimise for region overlap and do not directly penalise boundary
displacement.
Adding a Hausdorff-distance-based loss component \cite{karimi2020hausdorff}
alongside the existing Dice loss could encourage the network to reduce
large localised errors near object boundaries, potentially sharpening
predicted contours without sacrificing overall segmentation accuracy.

Finally, the authors themselves mention data scarcity as a challenge,
noting that expert annotation is expensive and that datasets like
DRIVE contain only 20 training images.
GAN-based synthetic augmentation \cite{shin2018synthesis} might be
useful here to complement the existing patch-based augmentation: a
conditional GAN could generate plausible retinal or lesion images with
paired segmentation masks, introducing more anatomical variety than
geometric transforms alone can provide.

To test whether these changes actually help, it would make sense to
run ablation experiments on the same datasets and splits as the
original paper, adding each component incrementally and measuring
Dice, sensitivity, and AUC, along with Hausdorff distance as an
additional boundary metric.
Qualitative comparison of predicted boundaries against ground truth
would also help assess whether sharpness visibly improves.
